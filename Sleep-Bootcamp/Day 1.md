<h2 id="wakefulness">Wakefulness</h2>
<p>The reticular activating system keeps you awake. It has several parts, and each part produces/responds to specific neurotransmitters.</p>
<p>Neruotransmitters of the ascending arousal system <strong>‚ÄúH-GONADS‚Äù</strong> Histamine, Glutamate, Orexin, Norepinephrine, Acetylcholine, Dopamine, Serotonin</p>
<h2 id="sleep">Sleep</h2>
<p>Mainly GABA which suppresses the above loci and thus induces sleep. This is driven by VLPO (ventrolateral preoptic nucleus)</p>
<h2 id="neuropharmacology-of-sleep">Neuropharmacology of Sleep</h2>
<ul>
<li>Slow Wave Sleep: inhibition of the arousal system mediated through GABA.</li>
<li>REM or Paradoxical Sleep: activation of cholinergic neurons, and inhibition of noradrenergic along with other neuroons that stimulate behavioral arousal.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="sleep-and-the-respiratory-system">Sleep and the Respiratory System</h2>
<p>CO2 increased during sleep (RR same, but less TV)</p>
<h2 id="sleep-and-thermoregulation">Sleep and Thermoregulation</h2>
<p>Body temperature normally falls at sleep onset.<br>
Thermoregulatory function is severely compromised in REM sleep. Exposing animal to hot or cold environments, suppresses sleep, and especially REM sleep.</p>
<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li>Sleep is a complex process that is mediated by suppression of the ascending arousal system (VLPO; GABA), and causes a series of dynamic changes leading to NREM and REM Sleep.</li>
<li>Sympathetic and Parasympathetic Autonomic Activity are affected by sleep stage and impact cardiac, pulmonary, and thermoregulatory function in normal individuals. Other organ systems and their functions are impacted upon by the autonomic nervous system as well.</li>
<li>Sleep deprivation, sleep disorder, and circadian rhythm are other factors that impact upon these processes.</li>
</ul>

<h1 id="approach-to-parasomnias-john-konikkara-md">Approach to Parasomnias: John Konikkara, MD</h1>
<h2 id="definition">Definition</h2>
<p>Abnormal sleep related complex movements, behaviors, emotions, perception, dreams, or autonomic nervous system activity.</p>
<h2 id="process-s-and-process-c">Process S and Process C</h2>
<p>Hemostatic - S, and Circadian - C<br>
<img src="https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Samuele_Cortese/publication/236390112/figure/fig1/AS:393369416159238@1470797985285/The-two-process-model-of-sleep-regulation.png" alt="S &amp; C Processes"></p>
<h2 id="nrem-parasomnias">NREM Parasomnias</h2>
<p>Rule out sleep deprivation, fragmentation, and substance use. Majority has no neurological or psychological pathology‚Ä¶<br>
Hypothesis is that they are secondary to the dissociation of different regions of the brain, associated with activation of locomotor centers</p>
<h3 id="confusional-arousal">Confusional arousal</h3>
<ul>
<li>General criteria for NREM plus</li>
<li>Mental confusions or confused behavior</li>
<li>Absence of terror</li>
<li>Treatment rarely indicated</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="sleepwalking">Sleepwalking</h3>
<ul>
<li>Gnereal criteria for NREM plus</li>
<li>Arousal are associated with ambulation and other complex behaviors out of bed</li>
<li>May result in agitation or violent behavior</li>
<li>Treatment is avoidance of precipitating factor, and scheduled awakening 15-30 minutes prior to typical episodes</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="sleep-terror">Sleep terror</h3>
<ul>
<li>General criteria for NREM plus</li>
<li>Abrupt terror or frightening scream</li>
<li>Signs of intense fear and autonomic arousal (tachycardia, and diaphoresis)</li>
<li>Treatment is reassurance of patients or parents</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="sleep-related-eating-disorder">Sleep related eating disorder</h3>
<ul>
<li>Recurrent episodes of dysfunctional eating that occur after an arousal during the main sleep period.</li>
<li>Usually associated with sleepwalking.</li>
<li>Can be triggered by medications such as Ambien.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="rem-parasomnias">REM Parasomnias</h2>
<h3 id="rem-behavior-disorder">REM Behavior Disorder</h3>
<ul>
<li>Repeated episodes of sleep related vocalization or complex motor behavior.</li>
<li>More in common in males.</li>
<li>May be seen associated with Parkinson‚Äôs, DLB, MSA, narcolepsy, and brain stem tumors.</li>
<li>RBD in children could be the initial sign of narcolepsy type 1.</li>
<li>Treatment: bedroom safety, clonazepam, and melatonin.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="nightmare-disorder">Nightmare Disorder</h3>
<ul>
<li>Repeated occurrences of extended, extremely dysphoric, and well-remembered dreams that usually involve threats to survival, security or physical integrity.</li>
<li>Treatment: image rehersal therapy, SBT, and systemic desensitization. Medications such as prazosin, trazodone, and atypical antipsychotics.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="other-parasomnias">Other parasomnias</h3>
<ul>
<li>Exploding head syndrome</li>
<li>Sleep enuresis</li>
</ul>
